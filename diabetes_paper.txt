DiabeRules: a transparent rule based expert
system for managing diabetes
Arpita Nath Boruah1* and Saroj Kumar Biswas2
Abstract
Diabetes, often referred to as a chronic disease, encompasses a group of metabolic disorders characterized by persistently elevated blood sugar levels. It is a major cause of death each year, with many individuals unaware of their
condition until it is too late. This long-term disease impairs the body’s ability to produce insulin, leading to excessive
blood sugar levels that can result in eye impairment, nerve damage, cardiovascular damage, kidney damage, and
stroke. Therefore, it is crucial to develop a system that can detect diabetes early, alerting individuals to the danger and
enabling them to take preventive measures to manage it. Early identification of risk factors can significantly reduce
the severity of the disease. Considering the severity of the disease and the research work done till date, this paper
introduces an expert system named DiabeRules: Diabetes Management using Comprehensible Rules. The system
employs a decision tree (DT) to identify the primary rules responsible for diabetes in detail. To derive promising rules,
a hybrid DT is created and further refined to eliminate irrelevant rules using a Sequential Hill Climbing approach with
a customizable heuristic function. The DiabeRules model ultimately generates a decision rule set that is transparent
and comprehensible, helping individuals understand how to manage diabetes. Using a diabetes dataset from the UCI
repository, the performance of the DiabeRules system is compared to that of current, recently developed systems. The
experimental results demonstrate that the proposed DiabeRules system is effective in managing diabetes by focusing
on essential rules and critical risk factors.
Keywords: Diabetes, Disease management, Machine learning, Decision tree, Rule based system, Hill climbing
approach, Heuristic search
Introduction

million individuals worldwide lose their lives to diabetes
each year. One condition that develops when blood glucose/blood sugar levels are extremely high in the body
is diabetes. Diabetes is one of the top ten leading causes
of death in 2016. Diabetes has surpassed HIV/AIDS as
the sixth leading cause of death. The global prevalence
of diabetes among individuals over 18 years of age has
increased from 4.7% in 1980 to 8.5% in 2014, with the
number of persons with diabetes rising from 108 million
in 1980 to 422 million in 2014. Diabetes is predicted to
affect 642 million persons (1 in 10) by 2040. In addition,

The decision tree (DT) is a common transparent
machine learning technique that creates clear rules for
a decision [9]. The knowledge learned by a DT is represented by these rules, which are also known as P-Rules
or Production Rules. DT can construct transparent decision rules with a combination of factors in the antecedent
part of the rules. However, it occasionally generates some
redundant and unnecessary rules, reducing its comprehensibility. In case of medical science to prevent a disease
like diabetes a clear explanation of the causes of the disease is required to spread awareness among the common
people and to take some precautionary measures to prevent/manage the disease in advance. Therefore, this paper
proposes an expert system for diabetes named, DiabeRules: Diabetes Management using Transparent Rules to
control and manage the disease with a few decision rules.

The proposed methodology of diabetes
management using transparent rules: DiabeRules
The proposed DiabeRules consists of 4 steps: FeatureSelection, Rule Generation, Weighted Rule Extraction
Rating and Weighted Rule Optimization. Figure 1 depicts
the proposed DiabeRules system’s framework. The Feature-Selection phase selects the best feature from the
set and the rule generation step produces rule-set using
DT; the Weighted Rule Extraction Rating step selects the
transparent rules among the rules generated by DT using
a scored function, the Weighted Rule Optimization step
eliminates the irrelevant and ineffective rules.
Feature‑selection
The Feature-Selection step consists of the proposed feature selection technique Stochastic Hill Climbing with K
nearest neighbor (SHCK), where Stochastic Hill climbing approach which is a heuristic search method is used
to select the most relevant and prominent features. Until
the predefined maximum iteration is reached the search
for the features continues. The SHCK algorithm searches
the best subset of features by removing the irrelevant
features from the current feature set. For removing a
feature, a fitness proportionate selection procedure with
a probability to select a feature proportional to its score
Page 4 of 15
is used. Since DT is used as the learning model, so the
score function used in the proposed algorithm is the
overall accuracy. Each of the features are clustered into
the number of classes for a particular dataset, thus setting the K value and a score value is assigned to them
based on their distance from the centroid of the cluster to
which they belong. The centroids of the K clusters are initially assigned with the random numbers chosen between
the minimum and maximum value of the class label. If
the class label is “0” and “1” then the random numbers
for the centroids are chosen between 0 and 1. Similarly
for continuous domain of class label the maximum and
minimum value of the domain will be taken as range for
generating the random numbers. This score value is used
in the fitness proportionate selection procedure. If the
new subset of feature improves the overall performance
of the learning model, that change is retained. Else, it is
discarded and then another neighbor in the feature space
is taken into consideration. So as to reduce the deficiency
of hill climbing due to incomplete search strategy SHCK
is repeated with a predetermined maximum number of
iterations. The feature selection procedure in algorithmic
form is given below.

Rule generation
The DiabeRules system uses the processed data to generate the P-Rules (decision rules) for Diabetes. It uses C4.5
algorithm [10] to generate the decision rules by calculating information gain ratio. The information gain ratio
helps in choosing the feature at each node in the tree for
splitting. The DT decision rules can be read from the root
node following the decision node to the leaf node which
represents the decision.

Risk factor analysis
The accuracy of the Intelligible Insight Rules is 89.23%.
The global and local comprehensibility of the Intelligible
Insight Rules Set is 4 and 12 (2 attributes with different
ranges) respectively. From Table 4, it is observed that in
the Intelligible Insight Rules, there are different ranges of
same features. Therefore, the 4 rules are merged as given
below:
Merged Rule: if (X2 ≤ 143.5 and X6 ≤ 27.35 and
X6 > 22.95) then class = 0
The main objective of the proposed DiabeRules model
is to identify the risk factors with ranges responsible
for diabetes to control and manage diabetes to a great
extent. To identify the risk factors from the optimised
and merged (single) rule, the sequential search algorithm
is used because the rule consists of 2 different attributes
and if the value of this 2 attributes are within the given
ranges, then a person belongs to a negative class, means
he/she is not suffering from diabetes. The algorithm evaluates each risk factor independently. Each risk factor presents in the merged rule is taken under consideration by
keeping the other risk factors inactive and the misclassification rate for the respective risk factor is calculated by
reversing its range. The risk factor which produces highest average misclassification rate for negative class, when
its range is reversed, is considered as the major risk factor. If more than one major risk factor needs to be identified, then the incremental Sequential Search process is
iteratively executed to identify the intended number of
major risk factors with ranges. Table 7 shows the rate of
misclassified patterns when the range of a particular single risk factor is reversed.

of its range gives a high misclassification rate. So, if the
scenario is to select single major factor responsible for
diabetes, then X6 is selected as the one. Now for more

Conclusion
This paper offers a machine learning algorithm-based
system named DiabeRules. The system finds the most
important and understandable decision rules for recognising diabetes, allowing for the prevention of diabetes to some extent. The proposed approach is tested
using a diabetic dataset from the University of California at Irvine’s repository. Using the DT algorithm,
the DiabeRules system generates effective decision
rules for determining the occurrence of diabetes. The
decision rules are then scored, and the insignificant/
irrelevant decision rules are pruned using a Sequential Hill Climbing technique to provide a reduced rule
set. And finally, the algorithm identifies the major risk
factor of diabetes disease. According to the results
of the experiments, Diabetes can be significantly
detected and controlled significantly by analysing 4
decision rules which comprises of 2 factors namely
Plasma glucose and BMI. The suggested system is
compared to an existing system, and the findings
reveal that the designed methodology is more effective in managing Diabetes than the existing method.
The proposed method’s accuracy under tenfold crossvalidation was 89.23%. In order to guarantee comparability with relevant studies and to establish a fair
foundation for benchmarking, this evaluation technique was selected. It should be noted, however, that
utilizing the same dataset for feature selection, rule
induction, and evaluation could result in overly optimistic estimations. Although tenfold cross-validation
is still a commonly used technique in the area, nested
cross-validation or independent test sets may be used
in subsequent research to offer a more thorough evaluation of generalization performance.
The presented work can be expanded upon by employing some pre-processing approaches to deal with the
Diabetes dataset’s imbalanced nature and improve its
usability. Optimising the decision rules and finding the
significant rules can be accomplished with the help of
additional algorithms.
